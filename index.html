Introduction

Fibropapillomatosis is a tumor causing disease taking the form of cauliflower like mutations on turtle's bodies. It poses a significant threat to sea turtle populations, particularly green sea turtles, as it ravages their health and raises concerns about the species' survival. This cancer, associated with a herpesvirus infection, has emerged as a leading cause of mortality among sea turtles, highlighting the urgent need for research and intervention to combat its devastating effects.

Research Hypothesis

Despite the severity of fibropapillomatosis, limited attention has been given to understanding its underlying mechanisms. In this study, the hypothesis is that similarities exist between fibropapillomatosis in sea turtles and certain human cancers, suggesting the potential for repurposing existing human cancer therapies to combat this disease in turtles. By exploring these parallels, the paper aims to identify novel therapeutic strategies that could aid in the conservation of sea turtle populations.

Methodology

To investigate the paper's hypothesis, they obtained RNA samples from juvenile green sea turtles affected by fibropapillomatosis. Through transcriptomic analysis, they discovered a distinct gene expression profile associated with tumor growth, particularly in epidermal and dermal cells. Notably, the findings revealed minimal viral activity within the tumors, suggesting alternative drivers of tumor progression.

Key Findings

Further analysis using ingenuity pathway analysis uncovered similarities between fibropapillomatosis tumors and human basal cell carcinoma (BCC), pancreatic cancer, and neuronal-derived cancers. This unexpected link prompted an investigation into potential environmental factors contributing to tumor development.

Environmental Influence

Further investigation revealed a mild correlation between UV exposure, exacerbated by climate change, and the prevalence of fibropapillomatosis tumors in green sea turtles. Drawing parallels with BCC in humans, which is heavily influenced by UV exposure, underscores the significance of environmental factors in cancer development across species.

Therapeutic Approach

Building upon the molecular understanding of fibropapillomatosis, therapeutic interventions adapted from human cancer treatments were explored . Remarkably, administering a BCC remedy, specifically 5-FU eye drops, resulted in significantly reduced tumor regrowth following surgical removal compared to untreated turtles. This highlights the potential efficacy of repurposed therapies in combating fibropapillomatosis in sea turtles.

Conclusion

This study sheds light on the molecular mechanisms driving fibropapillomatosis in sea turtles and underscores the value of interdisciplinary approaches in addressing wildlife diseases. By leveraging insights from human cancer research, new avenues for the conservation and management of sea turtle populations can be offered, emphasizing the interconnectedness of human and wildlife health.

Citation: Sea turtle fibropapilloma tumors share genomic drivers and therapeutic vulnerabilities with human cancers. Duffy et al., Communications biology, 1, 63 (2018)
